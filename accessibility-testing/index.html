Accessibility Testing: Plainsboro Public Library


LIS 4206: Web Content
Autumn Term 2025


Author: Paulina Cosma
Website: Plainsboro Public Library
Date: October 2025

About

Hello, my name is Paulina Cosma, and I am a MLIS student at the University of Denver, as well as a current volunteer at this library. This term, I am taking a Web Content course, and we are learning about the importance of digital accessibility and effective strategies to make web content more inclusive for people with disabilities, including those who use assistive technologies. We were asked to perform accessibility testing on a local organization’s website to identify potential issues that might prevent people with disabilities or those using assistive devices from accessing the information easily. I chose to test your website.


Did you know?
As of July 15, 2024, the CDC reported that more than one in four adults in the United States has a disability, representing 28.7% of the total population (CDC, 2024).


This report aims to help The Plainsboro Public Library better align with Section 508 and the Americans with Disabilities Act Title II Regulations. The accessibility checklist is based on the W3C Easy Checks. Assessment tools like WAVE and ANDI, along with keyboard testing, were used. I hope this report proves helpful. Thank you for reviewing my recommendations.

Pages Evaluated

These were the four website pages I focused my assessment on.

  • Book Club
  • Visually Impaired Services
  • Events & Bookings
  • Art Gallery

Accessibility Checklist

Page Titles: Needs Improvement

Page titles are important because they help all users navigate browser tabs when multiple webpages are open. More importantly, they are the first thing screen readers read to help users with disabilities in knowing where they are. The best way to make page titles accessible is to “front-load' critical or unique information first. Currently, your page titles read:

  • Book Club – Plainsboro Public library
  • Visually Impaired Services offered thru the Talking Book & Braille…
  • Events & Bookings | Plainsboro Public Library
  • Enjoy the Art Gallery Exhibits Presented by the Plainsboro Public…

I recommend updating your titles to match the approach used with your Book Club Webpage:

  • Visually Impaired Services - Plainsboro Public Library
  • Events & Bookings – Plainsboro Public Library
  • Art Gallery – Plainsboro Public Library

Image Text Alternatives: Needs Improvement

Text alternatives, also known as “alt text," are essential because they convey the purpose of images, such as pictures, illustrations, and other visual elements, to users who are unable to see them. This includes individuals using screen readers or those who disable images to speed up loading times or save bandwidth. The aim of this text is to provide an equivalent user experience, not just to describe the image.

While many of your pages use alt text to identify images, several do not. Additionally, the quality of the existing alt text on images that have it needs improvement. On your Book Club page, the linked images in the Upcoming Reads rotating image carousel lack alternative text. When displaying banners or posters, all information that someone without a visual disability can read on the image should also be accessible to patrons using screen readers, including event dates and times, not just the title, as I observed in your events carousel on the Events & Bookings page. Similarly, on your Events & Bookings page, two images have title attributes but no alt attributes. Additionally, on the Events & Bookings page, I noticed several table elements; none have headers, which could confuse users with screen readers, as all the information would appear as unstructured table data. The Art Gallery page uses ARIA labels in the image carousel; unfortunately, they are numbered and do not provide helpful information for screen reader users. I also noticed that your footer element is missing alt text for the consortium logo, as well as the logos for the Apple App Store and Google Play Store.

I recommend manually reviewing each page on your website to verify that your alt-text is accurate and provides sufficient, concise information about images. For images with title attributes but without alt text, consider adding an ARIA label to provide additional accessibility information. To improve your table, add a hidden table header that enables screen readers to better interpret the table data. In this ARIA label, you can include the text on your drop-down menu, such as aria-label="Select a Library." Also, based on how it appears on your site, consider removing the two dashes before and after the phrase, as screen readers will read it as "dash-dash Select a Library dash-dash." For your footer, specify what each image represents, such as "Stella Consortium logo,” “Apple App Store Logo,” and “Google Play Store Logo.” You might also consider linking to the Apple and Google stores.

Text:

Headings: Needs Improvement

Headings help users who cannot use a mouse or rely on screen readers understand the page's structure, similar to a table of contents. Screen readers also use headings to navigate through a page. Headings are organized hierarchically, and each page should contain only one level 1 heading.

After reviewing your pages, the headings need improvement. I noticed several level one headings in the footers, but there are no level one headings in the main content areas. As previously mentioned, only one level one element should be used per webpage to create a proper hierarchy for screen readers and make information easier to access. The presence of multiple level one elements within the footer may cause confusion for users utilizing screen readers, as this practice does not establish a clear hierarchical structure. Navigating such pages can be challenging for screen reader users because level one headings typically denote the primary purpose or topic of a page.Instead of using a level one heading in the main content section, level two tags are used for section titles, often with multiple level two elements being the only headings present. Similarly, having multiple level one elements in the footer can confuse users relying on screen readers, as it doesn’t establish a clear hierarchy. Navigating these pages could be frustrating or impossible because of the difficulty in telling apart the two level one elements. On your Art Gallery page, heading levels jump from level two to level four without an level three tag in between, which hurts accessibility by breaking the logical flow.

I recommend incorporating an level one header into your primary content, preferably as the initial element within that section. Additionally, guarantee that all headings are arranged in a logical sequence without omissions. Moreover, eliminate the level one elements from your footer and modify your CSS to enlarge the text or style it according to your preference, ensuring that accessibility for users with visual impairments remains uncompromised.

Contrast Ratio (color contrast): Needs Improvement

Color contrast usually refers to the difference between the background color and the text. Some people need enough contrast between the text and background to read comfortably. High contrast is essential for those with visual impairments and many older adults who have reduced contrast sensitivity. However, for people with reading disabilities, bright colors might be hard to read. For regular-sized text, a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 is recommended.

On the Book Club page:
The highlight color of your tabs in the drop-down menu does not meet the minimum contrast ratio, with a ratio of 1.63:1. The dates in your events carousel also fail to meet the minimum contrast ratio, with a ratio of 2.81:1. Additionally, the white text on the yellow background in your footer has a contrast ratio of 1.68:1.

On the Visually Impaired Services page:
The tab highlight color remains problematic, as does the white text on a yellow background in your footer. However, under Directory Services, your Visually Impaired Services link appears in a pale green with a 2.32:1 contrast ratio. It's also worth noting that clicking on that link takes you back to the same webpage you're currently viewing; removing it entirely might be a better option.

On the Events & Bookings page:
Any white text on the blue background and any blue text on the white background do not meet the minimum contrast ratio, instead reflecting a 3.66:1 ratio. Overall, this page has 109 contrast errors.
On the Art Gallery page:
The tab highlight color remains an issue, as does the white text on a yellow background in your footer. Another contrast problem is the links beneath your Latest Exhibit posters, which have a contrast ratio of 2.3:1.

I suggest downloading and using ANDI to review your pages, as this tool offers an accessible option that most closely aligns with your original color choice. You can then update your CSS to match these accessible color selections.

Zoom 200% Resize Text: Needs Improvement

Increasing the size of text and images to 200% or more is sometimes used to enhance readability for people with visual impairments. Users should be able to access all information or navigation options while zoomed in, as some disabilities prevent horizontal scrolling, and most people cannot effectively read text in this way.

Three of the four webpages I assessed resized themselves flawlessly. It was highly encouraging to observe. Unfortunately, your Events & Booking page experienced an issue where the Events by Category categories did not resize properly, resulting in continuous horizontal scrolling being necessary for users to access all categories.

My recommendation is to change the nav-tabs code from aria-orientation=“horizontal” to “vertical”. Although users still need to scroll, vertical scrolling is generally more accessible.

Mobile View: Needs Improvement

In this digital age, users can access your website from various devices, including their mobile phones. The mobile view is used to ensure that accessibility is maintained on smaller screens without compromising the website's integrity.

The Book Club and Visually Impaired Services page both looked great in the mobile view; they scaled down really well and retained their integrity when I zoomed in.

The Events & Bookings and Art Gallery pages, however, did not scale down well, requiring users to horizontally scroll to view the page. I believe this is a result of the carousels located at the tops of these pages. Furthermore, when you zoom in on these pages, the problem worsens.

I recommend moving the carousels away from the top of the page. Also, compare the code from the Book Club and Visually Impaired pages with that of the Events & Bookings and Art Gallery pages to identify what is affecting the mobile view.

Interaction:

Keyboard Access and Visual Focus: Needs Improvement

Keyboard focus acts as a visible signal, highlighting the element currently in focus on a webpage. Many people cannot use a mouse, and those who are visually impaired or have mobility impairments often depend on keyboard navigation or assistive technologies that use keyboard commands. For users relying on keyboards for navigation, it is crucial to clearly identify which element has focus.

On the Book Club, Visually Impaired Services, and Art Gallery pages, the visual focus disappears after navigating the drop-down menu. On the Book Club tab, it requires 23 tab presses for keyboard focus to return. On the Visually Impaired Services page, the Directory of Services is completely inaccessible when using only the keyboard. On the Art Gallery page, it takes 22 tab presses to restore continuous keyboard focus. On the Event & Bookings page, the 'Skip to Content' link does not send you to the main content as intended. Additionally, there is no way to skip tabbing through the Upcoming Events list, which covers six weeks of events. Finally, on this page, keyboard-only users cannot navigate through the Events by Category sections, since only “Featured” is highlighted.

I recommend reviewing your code to ensure that all interactive elements like buttons and links, have a tab index set to 0. On the Visually Impaired Services page, it appears that the links not accessible via keyboard control are all set to -1. Additionally, review your nav-link-category code on the Events & Bookings page and remove the role=“presentation” attribute, as this causes screen readers to skip this list item. Instead, use the code you have for the featured button and duplicate it, modifying it to match each category to ensure accessibility for keyboard-only users.

Skip to Main: Needs Improvement

A skip to main link offers faster and simpler access to the main page content for keyboard-only users, screen reader users, and other users of assistive technology. Without this link, users would have to navigate through all menu items and search boxes each time they want to reach the main content, which wastes time and effort.

All four web pages I evaluated included a “Skip to Main” feature in keyboard-only navigation; however, only three of them actually directed users to the main content of the page. The Events & Bookings page failed to direct users to the main content but was able to direct users to the footer content, which served as a secondary navigational option. When using the link to navigate to the main content, however, the user remained in the same position as they were in the events carousel.

I suggest adding a level one heading in the main content and including an anchor target for that section in your code.

Forms, Labels, and Errors: Needs Improvement

Form field labels refer to the descriptions positioned adjacent to form fields. They specify the type of information to be entered or the options to be selected, such as checkboxes. These labels are crucial for ensuring that all users understand how to interact effectively with a form.

The only form present on all the webpages I evaluated was the search bar located in the top right corner. On the Book Club, Visually Impaired Services, and Art Gallery pages, your search box appears to have duplicate IDs in the code, such as input id="SearchBox” and button id="SearchButton.” A div element separates them; however, only one ID should be used per page. It also lacks a proper label for the search text field, so screen readers would not understand how to interpret this field. You do have radio buttons labeled; however, a screen reader may not be able to decipher what the group is for without an aria-label or a fieldset with a legend.

My recommendation is to remove the duplicate block concerning the IDs, add a screen-reader-only hidden label to the search text field, and include a fieldset with a legend to help screen readers interpret the information more effectively.

General:

Moving, Flashing, or Blinking Content: Needs Improvement

Moving, flashing, or blinking content, such as carousels and videos, can be distracting. Users should have control over this moving content, as it can pose challenges for individuals with attention deficit disorder or visual processing issues. Accessibility concerns include people who read and process information slowly, have difficulty tracking moving objects, or struggle to focus on other parts of the webpage because of the moving content. Additionally, blinking content can trigger seizures in people with photosensitive epilepsy, especially if it flashes more than three times per second, covers a large part of the screen, and is bright enough.

Three of the four webpages I assessed had moving carousels, but only the Events & Bookings page had a pause button.

I suggest adding carousel buttons to all your carousels, similar to those on the Events & Bookings page. Using the existing code and modifying it if needed will be the easiest solution.

Multimedia (video, audio) Alternatives and Closed Captioning: Not Applicable

Multimedia alternatives and closed captioning help people who are blind or have low vision access visual information. Similarly, captions and text transcripts help people who are deaf or hard of hearing in accessing audio content. There are three types of multimedia alternatives to consider: captions, transcripts, and audio descriptions. Captions are written versions of speech and non-speech audio that are necessary to understand the video, and they are displayed alongside the video. Please note that automatic captions are rarely sufficient for accessibility because they often lack accuracy and proper grammar. Transcripts are written versions of speech and non-speech information from audio content, made available separately from the video. Audio descriptions provide visual details needed to comprehend the content, including text shown in the video.

Basic Structure Check: Needs Improvement

This check disables images and display styles to identify what a user using a screen reader or other assistive devices would “see” when interacting with a website. Once the webpage information is “linearized,” it becomes easier to identify where potential accessibility issues may occur.

On the Book Club, Visually Impaired Services, and Art Gallery page, the search box appears twice, once before the menu and again immediately after it. Also, the consortium logo, the Apple App Store, and the Google Play Store all appear at the end.

I recommend reviewing the section of code that precedes and ends the menu items so you can remove the redundancy that causes the user to return to the search box at the end of the menu. Similarly, in the footer, include alt text for the logos and consider adding links to the Apple App Store and Google Play Store, labeling them accordingly.

On the Book Club page, although images have been disabled, your top banner, which I had assumed was a photo, remains visible. Immediately following the "Upcoming Reads" paragraph, there is a significant gap with no text or pictures, preceded by a date, a time, a link, and then "History Room" with seven empty bullet points. "View Details" concludes the section. This pattern is repeated three times. The following pattern is essentially the same as the first, though there is now a poster “image” in the space where the large gap existed in the first pattern. This is repeated three times before repeating the same pattern, featuring ample blank space, three more times.

I suggest labeling your banner as an image and adding alternative text to it. Similarly, review your carousel code and remove any unnecessary elements. More importantly, make sure all links are properly labeled and identified, and label the posters as images with alt text.

On the Visually Impaired Services page the banner image continues to be visible when images are disabled, indicating that it is not properly labeled.

Be sure to label this element as an image and include alt text because screen readers and other assistive devices cannot interpret images that aren’t correctly labeled.

On the Events & Bookings page, parts of the Plainsboro Public Library logo are visible, along with posters in the events carousel. The "Filter by category” option appears twice, once in the upcoming event section and again lower on the page. Without headings near the other filter groupings, it's unclear whether this filter is redundant or intended for a different purpose. There's also an automated graphic that does not appear on the webpage when images and style sheets are enabled. Due to the improper formatting of what seems to be a calendar, the information is unclear for a significant portion of this linearized page. After the “Events by Category” hyperlink section, there are several incorrectly structured tables that lack headers, contain nonsensical content, and make it difficult to determine whether this is one table or multiple tables, especially considering that when the style sheet and images are enabled, multiple tables are present. Unfortunately, this page needs considerable improvement.

Label decorative images accordingly and provide appropriate alt text for all other images. Evaluate whether the 'Filter by category' is redundant and, if not, assign it to the correct section. Adding headings throughout this page would greatly enhance navigation and understanding. Review your code to identify that graphic and remove it; it’s not present on your styled page and is unnecessary. Format your calendar as a table with clear headers and scope. Include a caption that describes the table, such as the month and year. Do not merge or split cells, as this can confuse screen readers by disrupting the expected grid layout. Apply these guidelines to all tables on the page. Using semantic tags like table, th, and td helps screen readers correctly interpret the relationship between the cell and the header correctly.

On the Art Gallery page, twelve images from the carousel are displayed, with some being duplicates. This is followed by a paragraph about the library art gallery and then the “Our Latest Exhibits” section. In this last section, posters showcasing past exhibits are shown.

To make this page more accessible, label the images as such and add alt text to help screen readers understand the content on the page.

Summary

In conducting this evaluation, my goal was to enhance the accessibility of the Plainsboro Public Library for all community members. While your website needs some adjustments to achieve full accessibility, most of the pages I reviewed were well-designed. Some pages only require minor changes to ensure universal access, while others will require more extensive work. I appreciate all that you do for our community. I sincerely hope you find my assessment helpful. Thank you for your time and commitment to accessibility.
